## What's happening

Amazon is ending its commingling program -- also called "stickerless inventory" -- for FBA sellers effective March 31, 2026. If you sell on Amazon FBA, this affects your inventory prep, your labeling workflow, and potentially your margins.

This isn't a small policy tweak. Amazon is fundamentally changing how inventory is tracked in its fulfillment centers, and sellers who don't prepare will have inventory stranded as "defective."

## What commingling was

Commingling let Amazon pool identical products from different sellers into a single shared inventory bin. Products were tracked by manufacturer barcode (UPC/EAN) rather than by individual seller. When a customer ordered from Seller A, Amazon might ship Seller B's unit if it was physically closer.

The upside: faster delivery and lower prep costs for sellers (no FNSKU stickers needed).

The downside: if any seller in the pool sent counterfeit, damaged, or expired goods, those units could ship to customers who ordered from a legitimate seller. The negative reviews and IP complaints would land on the wrong seller's account.

This has been a persistent problem. A 2023 Marketplace Pulse analysis found more than 15% of counterfeit-related complaints in Amazon's beauty category were tied to commingled inventory. Amazon is finally killing the practice entirely.

## What changes on March 31

Two things happen simultaneously:

**1. Commingling ends.** Amazon will no longer pool identical products from different sellers. Every unit will be attributed to a specific seller account.

**2. New barcode rules take effect.** Who can use manufacturer barcodes versus who must use FNSKU barcodes is restructured:

**Brand owners** (with the "Brand Representative" selling role in Amazon Brand Registry) can continue using manufacturer barcodes. Amazon will use "virtual tracking" to attribute specific units to their account. This is actually a simplification -- no more FNSKU stickering needed. Amazon estimates this saves the industry $600 million per year in labeling costs.

**Resellers** (anyone NOT enrolled in Brand Registry as a Brand Representative, including authorized resellers) must use Amazon FNSKU barcode stickers on every unit, even if the product already has a manufacturer barcode. No exceptions.

**Products without manufacturer barcodes** require FNSKU labels regardless of seller type.

**Products where a single UPC maps to multiple ASINs** require FNSKU labels regardless of seller type.

## Who's most affected

**Maximum impact: wholesale and arbitrage resellers.** If you relied on stickerless commingling to avoid labeling costs and simplify prep, your entire workflow changes. Every unit now needs an FNSKU sticker.

**Moderate impact: resellers with existing FNSKU workflows.** You're already labeling, but verify all your SKUs are set correctly in Seller Central.

**Minimal impact: brand owners in Brand Registry.** You actually benefit -- you can stop applying FNSKU stickers on products with manufacturer barcodes. Cost savings.

**No impact: sellers of consumables, topicals, and other previously ineligible categories.** These products were already required to use FNSKU labels.

## What to do now

### If you're a brand owner

1. **Verify your Brand Representative status** in Amazon Brand Registry. This is what determines whether you qualify for manufacturer barcode tracking.
2. **For products with manufacturer barcodes**: you can stop applying FNSKU stickers after March 31. That's money saved.
3. **For products without manufacturer barcodes**: continue applying FNSKU labels.
4. **Review your catalog** for any barcode mapping issues, especially products where one UPC maps to multiple ASINs.

### If you're a reseller

1. **Switch all products to FNSKU barcode labeling now.** Don't wait until late March.
2. **Generate FNSKU labels** from Seller Central for each ASIN. Print on a thermal label printer (Zebra, DYMO, Rollo all work).
3. **Label specifications**: minimum 1" x 2", placed on the outside of the product where scannable, always cover or obscure the manufacturer barcode so Amazon's scanners only read the FNSKU.
4. **Set up a labeling process**: do it in-house, or outsource to a third-party FBA prep center (AMZ Prep, Marketplace Valet, and others handle this at scale).
5. **Factor increased costs into your margins.** Labels, labor, and time add per-unit cost. Re-evaluate which products remain profitable.

### All sellers

1. **Audit your current inventory settings** in Seller Central. Check the ASIN-level report Amazon has provided showing which products are affected.
2. **Update your shipping workflows and prep SOPs** before the deadline.
3. **Send a test shipment** with FNSKU labels now to confirm everything scans properly and gets checked in without issues.

## What happens if you miss the deadline

Inventory received from resellers without proper FNSKU barcodes after March 31 will be marked as **defective** by Amazon. Defective inventory won't be fulfilled to customers -- it's effectively stranded. Defect fees apply: $0.32 to $1.74 per unit for standard-sized products. No reimbursement for lost or damaged units that weren't properly labeled.

The inventory won't be destroyed, but it becomes unusable until properly labeled, which requires a removal order and re-shipment. That's time, money, and stockouts.

## Important context: FBA Prep Service already ended

Amazon already ended its FBA Prep and Labeling Service on January 1, 2026. Amazon no longer labels products for sellers. If you were relying on Amazon to apply FNSKU labels for you, that option is gone. You must handle all labeling yourself or use a third-party prep service.

## The bigger picture

Amazon has been moving toward eliminating commingling for years. The counterfeit problem was too visible and too damaging to the platform's trust. This change protects brand owners and legitimate sellers, while adding operational burden to resellers.

If you're a reseller, this is a cost of doing business on the platform going forward. Build it into your unit economics. If it makes certain low-margin products unprofitable, better to know now than to have inventory stranded in April.

## Timeline

- **January 1, 2026**: FBA Prep and Labeling Service ended
- **March 31, 2026**: Commingling ends, new barcode rules take effect
- **Existing inventory**: commingled stock already in FBA warehouses can sell through as-is. The rules apply only to new shipments after March 31.

Don't wait until the last week. FBA prep centers will be overwhelmed near the deadline. Start transitioning now.
